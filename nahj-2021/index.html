Some thoughts on dataviz

1. Garbage in, garbage out. A nice-looking graphic won't save a set of lousy data.

2. Keep it consistent. Things that are the same should look the same.

3. Everybody loves maps, but not every dataset involving states or counties â€“or other geographic dataâ€“ needs to be a map.

-When maps shouldn't be maps, by Matthew Ericson

4. Colors are great, but too many colors can be overwhelming and make your graphic hard to read. Keep your palettes simple. Use color to stress what it's important.

-More on colors, from Datawrapper's blog

-More on colors, by Claus Wilke

-Good collection of gradients, from Carto

-More palettes

-Tools to test if your data visualization is colorblind friendly Color Oracle and Sim Daltonism [only for Mac]

-Tool to find good combinations of colors, Adobe

5. Sketch by hand before using any software. Think about what data you have in your dataset (time, geography, multiple variables).

6. You don't need to put everything in one graphic. Sometimes, the best solution to explain something is to use more than one graphic.

7. Learn how to use graphics. Don't choose graphics because of their aesthetics. First, think about what you expect your audience to learn from that graphic and what chart fits better with your data.

    1. Download the data from here. [IMPORTANT: Don' try to open it with Excel or Numbers]

    2. Go to Datawrapper and click on 'New map' in the menu at the top. Then select the 'Choropleth map' option.

    3. In the search area, type 'USA' and select USA counties (2018). Click on 'Proceed.'

    4. The next step allows you to copy and paste the data or upload a file. My recommendation, in this case, is: upload the file.
